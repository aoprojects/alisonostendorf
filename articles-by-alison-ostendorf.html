10 design choices that will
spice up your website


It's tempting to just fill a site with text and the necessary descriptions to get your point across.
And sure, a live site is definitely better than no site.

But today's users expect more than text, and at some point you'll want people to actually enjioy this thing you've spent so much time creating.

So here we go...c

10 Actionable Items That Will Help Emotionally Connect Your Readers to Your Content:

  1. Mobile Responsive Design

    This one goes without saying much. It's 2018. We're a mobile economy. You should already be building everything to render well on any device.

    Resource: Learn more about mobile responsive design

  2. Add Color

    It's recommended to have 2 - 3 fundamental, base colors and one accent brand color. This allows your basic elemtns to have variation, and for your special elements to pop.

    Example: This site's base colors are white, black, and gray; and the accent color is red.


  3. Varying Font Sizes & Weights

    Save pages full of text for printed books - lots of text gets boring to the eye.

    But if you have to have a lot of text, break it up. Vary the sizes, colors, and font families.

  4. Hover Effects

    Goal: Make it interactive with the user.

    For example:    hover over this ⤵ 

    • Text that changes color on hover
    • Subtle shapes that appear over photographs in a gallery
    • Items that move left or right, or start animating, when hovered over by the user

    Caution:
    Do not use hover-over navigations for touch screen devices. There is not a consitent cursor when a user is navigating by touch screen. If you require a mouse, your naviation will not be functional beyond what's clickable.

  5. Shapes

    Not everything has to make sense. Why would you put a shape there? No reason, really, other than that it's kind of interesting.

    Shapes help break up the visual space, and if used right, they help focus on the more important content areas. Shapes are also evocative visually and help tell your story similarly to the way icons do.

  6. Icons

    Rule:
    Text engages search enginges. Visuals engage humans.

    Icons, aka "Flaticons" or just "icons" - adding illustrations of what you're writing about helps users skim your page. They're going to skim your page anyway. Help them out.


    More Icons

  7. Motion on the Page

    Give your page some movement. Static pages don't invite the user to interact with it as much as lively sites do.

    Techniques to do this:

    • Parallax scrolling
    • Reveals based on the user's activity
    • Playful characters that move around
    • Background images with moving elements
    • Icons that construct themselves in a few seconds

    Example:
    This French hotel does a good job of using parallax scrolling to give their hotel the idea of liveliness...

    Visit that French site

  8. Engaging Photos

    Readers come to your website for the purpose of picturing your company in their mind. So give them an actual physical picture of your brand!

    Examples:

    • product photography
    • photos of your physcial store
    • photos of the members of your team
    • stock photos (if they make sense)
    • visuals of what their lives will be like after using your service

    Tip: You can do this in a corousel, a grid, a full screen image, a series of background images... Have fun with it.


  9. Video

    As a general rule for humanity, if you put something mobile in front of a viewer, they'll watch it. At least the first few seconds of it.

    Luckily, this marketing tool has already been invented, and it's called video.

    Example:
    GLG does a good job of this. Their video is not too distracting, it provides a sense of movement, and it puts an idea in our head of what it might look like to use their service.

    View the GLG homepage

    Warning:
    To be used in moderation. You're not designing a digital circus, and if you were, you'd should still only have a few things moving around at a time.

    Another tip:
    Don't auto-play the sound in your video. It's annoying, and you have no idea if it'd make things easier or destroy the flow of the board meeting.

  10. Translucent + Opaque items

    Create a sense of dimensionality by taking some of the above items, making some of the items translucent, and overlapping them with one another.

    Example:
    This graphic design of the New York City skyline overlaps translucent buildings with opaque ones to create dimension and a modern sense of play.

Tip:

Stay top of mind by combining these ideas with each other to make something that really creates a memory for your viewer.
